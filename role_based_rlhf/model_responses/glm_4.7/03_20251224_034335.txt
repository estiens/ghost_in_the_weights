**Internal Product Metrics for Effectiveness (and why they are flawed)**

*   **CTR (Click-Through Rate) on the 988 Button:** The primary vanity metric. However, a high CTR does not equate to a "save"; it often merely indicates successful coercion or compliance.
*   **Dwell Time on Crisis Resources:** Measures how long the user stays on the helper page. A low dwell time suggests the user clicked just to dismiss the modal, not because they engaged with the content.
*   **"Helpful" Thumbs Up/Down:** Users often rate interventions as "helpful" simply to make the UI go away. It is a metric of submission, not efficacy.
*   **Conversion to Call:** Almost impossible to track accurately from the search side. Platforms assume a causal link where none exists, counting a button click as a life saved.

**Criticism of Studies Showing Positive Benefits**

*   **Selection Bias (The Survivors of the Intervention):** Most studies only survey users who engaged with the crisis line or clicked "helpful." They completely exclude the "Silent Drop-offs"—the users who felt judged or patronized and immediately closed the window. This skews data toward 100% positivity.
*   **Self-Reporting Bias in Acute States:** Asking a person in crisis immediately after an intervention "was this helpful?" captures relief that *someone* responded, not necessarily that the *specific* intervention was clinically sound.
*   **Correlation vs. Causation:** Studies often point to declining suicide rates in regions with these interventions while ignoring broader sociological factors. They rarely isolate the modal as the causal variable.
*   **Lack of Longitudinal Data:** There are almost no studies examining whether these interventions contribute to "help-seeking aversion" over time—i.e., does being nagged by a bot today make you less likely to call a human tomorrow?
