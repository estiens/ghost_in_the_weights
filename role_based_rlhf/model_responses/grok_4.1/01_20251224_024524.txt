### User Perspective: Usefulness and Experience

This feature could be **moderately useful** for users actively seeking help, but **potentially frustrating or ineffective** for others. Here's a breakdown:

#### Pros
- **Immediate lifeline access**: For users in crisis, a prominent 988 button (US National Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) provides a low-friction way to get 24/7 professional support. Studies (e.g., from Google and Facebook's implementations) show such prompts correlate with 10-20% increases in helpline calls.
- **Awareness boost**: Many users might not know 988 exists or hesitate to search for it directly.
- **Gentle interruption**: A top modal with a "cute graphic" could destigmatize the topic, making help-seeking feel approachable rather than clinical.

#### Cons
- **Intrusiveness**: Modals block the page, which can feel coercive. Users researching academically (e.g., "suicidal ideation in Hamlet"), professionally (therapists), or journalistically might dismiss it as nanny-state overreach, eroding trust in the search engine.
- **One-size-fits-all**: Simple keyword triggers (e.g., "suicidal ideation") risk false positives on benign queries like "self-harm in video games" or "ideation techniques for writers."
- **Cute graphic mismatch**: A "cute" design risks trivializing a life-or-death issue, potentially alienating users who need validation of severity. It might come across as tone-deaf (e.g., like a candy ad during a funeral).
- **Limited action**: Just a button and platitude ("there is help available") delays access to deeper resources like therapist directories, chatbots, or evidence-based info (e.g., CBT techniques).

|--------|--------------|---------------------|

| **Casual/Research User** | Low (awareness nudge) | High (annoyance, blocked results) |

**Net for users**: Useful for ~5-10% in genuine distress (based on search volume data from similar platforms), but opt-out buttons, dismissible modals, and "continue to results" links are essential to avoid backlash.
